**exact classes** from `cumulative_notebook.ipynb` and the **exact optimiser config**

optimisers train on the identical spiral dataset (`spiral_data(samples=100, classes=3)`,

pip install numpy nnfs

## SGD is slow, NOT stuck (extended run)

Running the Part 22 config (`SGD lr=1.0`) far past 10 000 epochs shows it keeps improving — it is not trapped in a local minimum:

| epoch | loss | acc |
|--:|--:|--:|
| 10 000 | 0.87 | 64.7% |
| 20 000 | 0.46 | 81.0% |
| 30 000 | 0.38 | 84.3% |
| 50 000 | 0.29 | 90.7% |

So the optimiser series should be framed around **convergence speed**, not "escaping local minima": vanilla SGD reaches a good region eventually (~91% by 50k epochs), but Adam/momentum get there in 10k. The "stuck in a local minimum" language in Parts 22-23 was incorrect and has been replaced.

(Also note the real SGD trajectory is a slow grind: loss 1.10 → 1.06 (1k) → 0.97 (5k) → 0.87 (10k), accuracy 36% → 40% → 51% → 65%, not the fast-then-stall curve the old Part 22 table showed.)

### Key correction for Part 23
Learning-rate decay does **not** lift spiral accuracy from 57.3% to 71.7%. With the
canonical config the final accuracy is essentially unchanged (~65% either way); decay's
real, demonstrable benefit is a **lower final loss (0.87 → 0.76)** and a **smoother,
less oscillatory loss curve**, plus a small peak-accuracy gain (67% → 69%). The post's
narrative should be reframed around loss/stability, not a large accuracy jump — or use a
configuration that genuinely showcases decay.

## Regularisation results (Parts 30-31 + Regularization dashboard)

From [`regularization_results.py`](regularization_results.py) — Adam(lr=0.05, decay=1e-5); regularisation on `dense1` only, applied to BOTH weights and biases (`weight_regularizer + bias_regularizer = 5e-4`, matching Parts 30/31 exactly); 10 000 epochs; test = a fresh spiral draw, dropout off. `train(drop)` = training accuracy with dropout ON (handicapped, what Part 31 quotes); `train(full)` = dropout off.

**100 samples/class:**

| Config | train(drop) | train(full) | test | gap (full−test) |
|---|--:|--:|--:|--:|
| No reg | 95.3% | 95.3% | 78.7% | 16.6 |
| L1 (5e-4) | 96.3% | 96.3% | 80.3% | 16.0 |
| **L2 (5e-4)** | 95.3% | 95.3% | **84.0%** | 11.3 |
| L1 + L2 (5e-4) | 82.0% | 82.0% | 80.3% | **1.7** |
| Dropout (0.1) | 65.0% | 72.0% | 64.7% | 7.3 |
| L2 + Dropout (0.1) | 75.4% | 79.0% | 69.3% | 9.7 |

**1000 samples/class:**

| Config | train(drop) | train(full) | test | gap (drop−test) |
|---|--:|--:|--:|--:|
| No reg | 90.7% | 90.7% | 87.7% | +3.0 |
| L2 (5e-4) | 90.1% | 90.1% | 88.5% | +1.6 |
| L2 + Dropout (0.1) | **68.8%** | 75.2% | **72.5%** | **−3.7** |

**Findings (config matters — reg must be on `dense1` only, on weights AND biases):**
- **Weight-decay regularisers help.** L2 is best (test 78.7% → 84.0%); L1 and L1+L2 also lift test (~80%); L1+L2 gives the smallest gap (1.7) with strong test accuracy. This confirms Part 30 ("L2 improves the gap without harming training").
- **Dropout over-regularises this small 64-neuron net** (test drops to ~65–69%); it needs more data or a wider layer to pay off.
- **Part 31's "negative gap" is CONFIRMED.** At 1000 samples, L2+Dropout gives handicapped `train(drop)`=68.8% < `test`=72.5% → negative gap, exactly as Part 31 describes (validation beats the dropout-handicapped training accuracy). With dropout OFF the training fit is 75.2% (gap is positive). The negative gap is a property of *which* training accuracy you quote.
- An EARLIER verification mistake applied L2 to both layers / weights-only and wrongly showed regularisation hurting — corrected here.
